How to learn systematically

This article was last updated: 5 hours ago

Here I want to write in detail about my personal systematic learning method. Generally prefer individual self-study.

1. Input and thinking

There are many books in this world, and it is impossible for us to finish reading every book. Of course, books can be good or bad. Picking out a good book or the book you want is trying to read a book roughly. Rough reading can also be understood as fast reading. The main purpose is to simply look through the table of contents, the table of contents, pictures, and tables in the book, and to get a general understanding of the content, key points and points of interest in the book. Sometimes reading personal information about the author can also help you understand the nature of a book better.

When you find a book then everything is ready. In the process of reading, at least for text books, I personally like so-called intensive reading. This is the process of reading a book from thin to thick. This means that every time you read a book, you are not just reading the superficality of the book, but trying to understand and think about the meaning behind each word. Whenever you don’t understand, look up relevant information and leave notes in the book. So slowly you will find that you have read this book thickly.

2. Notes and summary

In terms of notes, I have tried many different solutions. From the paper notes to the electronic notes. From messy methods to Cornell notes. From this, I slowly discovered a system of my own.

At this stage is a process of reading the book from thick to thin. Simply, it is to digest the knowledge you have learned and retell it in the most brief and easy way. So notes are particularly important. I personally use the form of electronic notes, it is a combination of the so-called Ipad + Apple Pencil. In this respect, I think electronic notes have more advantages than traditional notes. First, electronic notes are easier to organize and carry. The second is its versatility and convenience, so there is no need to bring a lot of crayons and so on. Of course, it also has disadvantages. Compared with traditional notes, writing experience and dependence are incomparable.

I personally like to write on notability. In taking notes, I use Cornell’s method.

3. Emphasis and improvement

When you finish taking notes, then you can understand the key points and essence of this book.

4. Output and reflection

In this regard, I personally think Feynman’s learning method is a very good method. You are in an input state during your learning process. Then the Feynman learning method is an output state. If a person understands the input but can’t express himself simply to express what he has learned, I think it is a pity.

“The person who says he knows what he thinks but cannot express it usually does not know what he thinks.” — Mortimer Adler

Richard Feynman, the late Nobel Laureate in physics, was once asked by a Caltech faculty member to explain why spin one-half particles obey Fermi Dirac statistics. Rising to the challenge, he said, “I’ll prepare a freshman lecture on it.” But a few days later he told the faculty member, “You know, I couldn’t do it. I couldn’t reduce it to the freshman level. That means we really don’t understand it.”

As Feynman once said, if a person cannot simply express what he has learned, then he does not understand it at all. Feynman’s learning method is not only an output function, it also has a function that allows you to reflect on what you have learned.

The Feynman learning method is divided into the following four steps:

  1. Be clear about what you want to learn
  2. Use simple language to tell what you have learned.
  3. If you get stuck in the process of telling, review what you have learned
  4. Make your explanation easier to understand, and the language becomes more simplified

The ultimate goal of Feynman’s learning method is to allow you to use your own language to explain what you have learned instead of those hard scientific terms.
